▸ What is WCAG, and what do AA / AAA / the ratios mean? — read this if the grades on this page are new to you

WCAG (Web Content Accessibility Guidelines) is the standard maintained by the W3C for making digital content usable by people with low vision, colour blindness, glare on their screen, an older phone in bright sunlight, or simply tired eyes at the end of the day. The part this tool checks is contrast: how much a piece of text or a UI element visually separates from the colour behind it.

Contrast is expressed as a ratio from 1:1 (identical colours, invisible) to 21:1 (pure black on pure white, maximum separation). It's calculated from the relative luminance of both colours — not how they look to a particular eye, but a fixed formula in the spec, so it's reproducible and testable. Every ratio on this page is measured against the colour a token actually sits on, not a generic assumption.

WCAG defines pass/fail thresholds at two levels:

AAA≥ 7.0:1 AA≥ 4.5:1 AA·Large≥ 3.0:1 Fail< 3.0:1

AA is the level most products, procurement checklists, and accessibility laws reference — treat it as the practical floor for body text and links. AAA is the stricter, enhanced level; it's harder to hit with saturated brand colours and isn't usually required, but is worth reaching for wherever it doesn't fight the design. Large text (roughly 18pt / 24px regular, or 14pt / 18.5px bold) and non-text UI elements like button outlines, icons, and input borders get a relaxed 3:1 minimum, because bigger or simpler shapes stay legible at lower contrast. Anything below 3:1 is a genuine accessibility failure — some readers won't be able to make it out at all.

Every text-bearing token this tool generates is automatically nudged until it clears its target: body text aims for AAA (7:1), muted/secondary text and coloured links aim for AA (4.5:1), and solid UI fills like buttons aim for the 3:1 component minimum. Non-text tokens — raw backgrounds, dividers, tinted fills — aren't graded against text thresholds since nothing is read directly off them.

Note: this tool measures WCAG 2.x contrast, which is the current legal and industry standard. A newer model called APCA (used in the draft WCAG 3) predicts perceived contrast more accurately in some cases, particularly for light text on dark backgrounds, but isn't yet the ratified standard — worth knowing if a ratio here feels stricter or looser than a colour looks in practice.
